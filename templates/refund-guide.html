IEEPA Tariff Guide

How to claim back IEEPA duties after the Supreme Court ruling

A practical guide for importers. What happened, who qualifies, what to file, and when — without the legal jargon.

Last updated: March 4, 2026

What happened

On February 20, 2026, the Supreme Court ruled 6–3 that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) does not give the President authority to impose tariffs. The decision — authored by Chief Justice Roberts and joined by Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, Gorsuch, Barrett, and Jackson — immediately invalidated two categories of tariffs:

Fentanyl/trafficking tariffs — imposed on China (starting Feb 2025), Canada and Mexico (from Mar 2025) — citing drug trafficking and immigration emergencies.

Reciprocal tariffs — imposed on virtually all trading partners from "Liberation Day" (April 2025) onward, with rates ranging from 10% to 145%.

The Court held that while IEEPA grants power to "regulate importation," this does not encompass the power to tax. Congress never delegated tariff authority through IEEPA — unlike Section 232 or Section 301, which explicitly do.

Key distinction: Only IEEPA tariffs were struck down. Section 232 (steel/aluminum), Section 301 (China tech), and standard MFN tariffs remain fully in force. A new Section 122 temporary 15% global surcharge was also imposed the same day — valid for 150 days.

Who qualifies for a refund

Refunds go to the importer of record — the party that filed entry documents with CBP and paid the assessed duties. This is typically the U.S. company that imported the goods, not the foreign supplier.

If a customs broker or freight forwarder served as importer of record on your behalf, the refund technically goes to them first. You'll need to coordinate with your broker to ensure those funds are passed through to you.

More than 301,000 importers paid IEEPA duties through mid-December 2025 across approximately 34 million customs entries. Total collections reached an estimated $175 billion.

Important: The Supreme Court did not order refunds. It ruled the tariffs unlawful, which opens the door — but the mechanics of refunds are being determined by CBP, the Court of International Trade, and potentially Congress. The administration has indicated it may resist automatic refunds.

Path 1 — Unliquidated entries

If your entry hasn't been liquidated yet (finalized by CBP), you have the simplest path to a refund. As of December 2025, roughly 19.2 million of the 34 million IEEPA entries were still unliquidated.

Liquidation typically occurs 314 days after entry. So goods imported after roughly April 2025 likely remain open.

1

Confirm your entry is unliquidated

Log into the ACE Secure Data Portal at ace.cbp.dhs.gov and pull your entry summary. Check the liquidation status. If it says "open" or "estimated," you can proceed with a PSC.

2

File a Post-Summary Correction (PSC)

Through the Automated Broker Interface (ABI) within ACE, file a PSC that recalculates duties by removing the IEEPA tariff component. The PSC must be filed within 300 days of entry or 15 days before the scheduled liquidation — whichever comes first. Your customs broker can handle this.

3

Separate IEEPA from surviving tariffs

This is critical. Your PSC should only remove tariffs under HTS headings 9903.01.xx (the IEEPA-specific codes). Section 232, Section 301, and regular MFN duties must remain. If you accidentally remove those, CBP will reject the correction or you'll face penalties.

4

Receive your refund via ACH

Since February 6, 2026, all CBP refunds are issued electronically via Automated Clearing House (ACH). Make sure you're enrolled at pay.gov. Funds arrive 1–2 business days after approval.

Path 2 — Already-liquidated entries

If CBP has already finalized your entry, the process is more involved. You have two options, and trade attorneys are recommending a "belt and suspenders" approach — do both.

Option A: File a CBP Protest (Form 19)

You have exactly 180 days from the liquidation date to file a protest. This is done through the ACE protest module using CBP Form 19. There are no extensions.

The protest should cite the Supreme Court's ruling in Learning Resources v. Trump and identify the specific IEEPA tariff headings (9903.01.xx) being contested. CBP has up to 2 years to review a protest, but you can request "accelerated disposition" — if they don't respond within 30 days, the protest is deemed denied, which opens the door to CIT litigation.

Deadline alert: The earliest IEEPA entries (February 2025, China fentanyl) began being liquidated in December 2025. That means 180-day protest windows start closing in June 2026. If you imported from China in early 2025, act now.

Option B: File at the Court of International Trade

For larger refund amounts, filing a summons at the CIT under 28 U.S.C. §1581(i) preserves your claim even if CBP denies the protest. The filing fee is approximately $400.

Nearly 2,000 importers had already filed protective CIT actions before the Supreme Court decision. The CIT has indicated it has authority to order reliquidation of entries — even those past the 180-day protest window — though whether it will exercise that power broadly remains to be seen.

If CBP denies your protest, you have another 180 days from the denial notice to file a CIT summons.

What tariffs still remain

The IEEPA ruling did not wipe the slate clean. These tariffs are still in force and were imposed under separate legal authorities:

Section 301 — China (7.5–25%)

Tariffs on Chinese goods originally imposed under the Trade Act of 1974 in response to intellectual property practices. Covers hundreds of billions in imports including electronics, apparel, and industrial goods. These predate IEEPA and remain fully active.

Section 232 — Steel (25%) & Aluminum (10–25%)

National security tariffs on steel and aluminum imports from most countries. Also now expanded to autos and auto parts (25%) as of March 2025. Not affected by the IEEPA ruling.

Section 122 — Temporary 15% surcharge

Imposed the same day as the IEEPA ruling. Applies globally at 15%. Limited to 150 days without Congressional action — set to expire around July 2026. Exceptions exist for USMCA goods, Section 232 products, and certain CAFTA-DR textiles.

Standard MFN duties

The regular Most Favored Nation tariff rates in the Harmonized Tariff Schedule. These vary by product and have been in effect for decades. Not changed by any recent action.

The net effect: The Treasury Secretary has stated that combining Section 122, 232, and 301 tariffs aims to produce "virtually unchanged tariff revenue in 2026." Importers should not expect lower costs overall — but they are legally owed back the IEEPA portion specifically.

Action checklist

Here's what to do this week, regardless of your entry status:

1

Pull your ACE entry data

Generate a report of all entries from February 2025 onward. Note the liquidation status and dates for each. If you don't have ACE access, request this from your customs broker or file a FOIA request with CBP.

2

Separate IEEPA from other tariffs

For each entry, identify the IEEPA tariff headings (9903.01.xx) and calculate the IEEPA-specific portion versus Section 232, 301, and MFN duties. Our refund estimator can help with rough numbers.

3

File PSCs on unliquidated entries

This is the fastest refund path. Have your broker file Post-Summary Corrections through ACE/ABI immediately for all open entries.

4

File protests on liquidated entries

For already-liquidated entries, file CBP Form 19 protests before the 180-day deadline. Prioritize the oldest entries first — their windows close soonest.

5

Enroll in ACH

All refunds are now electronic. Register at pay.gov if you haven't already.

6

Consider CIT protective filing

For significant refund amounts (typically $50K+), consult a trade attorney about filing a protective action at the Court of International Trade. This costs ~$400 and preserves your claims if administrative channels fail.

7

Keep paying current tariffs

Even though IEEPA tariffs are revoked, Section 122, 232, 301, and MFN tariffs are still due. Failure to pay current duties will jeopardize your bond and import privileges.
