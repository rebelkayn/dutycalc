Background

What happened

On February 20, 2026, the Supreme Court ruled 6–3 that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) does not give the President authority to impose tariffs. The decision — authored by Chief Justice Roberts — immediately invalidated two categories of tariffs:

Fentanyl/trafficking tariffs — imposed on China (starting Feb 2025), Canada and Mexico (from Mar 2025) — citing drug trafficking and immigration emergencies.

Reciprocal tariffs — imposed on virtually all trading partners from "Liberation Day" (April 2025) onward, with rates ranging from 10% to 145%.

The Court held that while IEEPA grants power to "regulate importation," this does not encompass the power to tax. Congress never delegated tariff authority through IEEPA — unlike Section 232 or Section 301, which explicitly do.

Key distinction: Only IEEPA tariffs were struck down. Section 232 (steel/aluminum), Section 301 (China tech), and standard MFN tariffs remain fully in force. A new Section 122 temporary 10% global surcharge was also imposed the same day — valid for 150 days.
Eligibility

Who qualifies for a refund

Refunds go to the importer of record — the party that filed entry documents with CBP and paid the assessed duties. This is typically the U.S. company that imported the goods, not the foreign supplier.

If a customs broker or freight forwarder served as importer of record on your behalf, the refund technically goes to them first. You'll need to coordinate with your broker to ensure those funds are passed through to you.

More than 301,000 importers paid IEEPA duties through mid-December 2025 across approximately 34 million customs entries. Total collections reached an estimated $175 billion.

Important: The Supreme Court did not order refunds. It ruled the tariffs unlawful, which opens the door — but the mechanics of refunds are being determined by CBP, the Court of International Trade, and potentially Congress. The administration has indicated it may resist automatic refunds.

Path 1

Unliquidated entries

If your entry hasn't been finalized by CBP, you have the simplest refund path. As of December 2025, roughly 19.2 million of the 34 million IEEPA entries were still unliquidated. Liquidation typically occurs 314 days after entry — so goods imported after roughly April 2025 likely remain open.

1

Confirm your entry is unliquidated

Log into the ACE Secure Data Portal at ace.cbp.dhs.gov and pull your entry summary. If status shows "open" or "estimated," you can proceed with a PSC.

2

File a Post-Summary Correction (PSC)

Through the Automated Broker Interface (ABI) within ACE, file a PSC that recalculates duties by removing the IEEPA tariff component. Must be filed within 300 days of entry or 15 days before scheduled liquidation — whichever comes first.

3

Separate IEEPA from surviving tariffs

Your PSC should only remove tariffs under HTS headings 9903.01.xx (IEEPA-specific codes). Section 232, Section 301, and regular MFN duties must remain. Accidentally removing those will result in rejection or penalties.

4

Receive your refund via ACH

Since February 6, 2026, all CBP refunds are issued electronically. Make sure you're enrolled at pay.gov. Funds arrive 1–2 business days after approval.

Path 2

Already-liquidated entries

If CBP has already finalized your entry, the process is more involved. Trade attorneys recommend a "belt and suspenders" approach — do both options below.

Option A: File a CBP Protest (Form 19)

You have exactly 180 days from the liquidation date to file a protest — no extensions. Use the ACE protest module, cite the Supreme Court ruling in Learning Resources v. Trump, and identify the specific IEEPA tariff headings (9903.01.xx).

CBP has up to 2 years to review. You can request accelerated disposition — if they don't respond within 30 days, the protest is deemed denied, which opens the door to CIT litigation.

Deadline alert: The earliest IEEPA entries (February 2025, China fentanyl) began being liquidated in December 2025. That means 180-day protest windows start closing in June 2026. If you imported from China in early 2025, act now.

Option B: File at the Court of International Trade

For larger refund amounts, filing a summons at the CIT under 28 U.S.C. §1581(i) preserves your claim even if CBP denies the protest. The filing fee is approximately $400.

Nearly 2,000 importers had already filed protective CIT actions before the Supreme Court decision. If CBP denies your protest, you have another 180 days from the denial notice to file a CIT summons.

What remains

Tariffs still in force

The IEEPA ruling did not wipe the slate clean. These are still active:

Section 301 — China (7.5–25%)

Tariffs on Chinese goods under the Trade Act of 1974, targeting IP practices. Covers electronics, apparel, and industrial goods. Predate IEEPA and remain fully active.

Section 232 — Steel (25%) & Aluminum (10–25%)

National security tariffs on steel and aluminum from most countries. Also expanded to autos and auto parts (25%) as of March 2025. Not affected by the IEEPA ruling.

Section 122 — Temporary 10% surcharge

Imposed the same day as the IEEPA ruling. Applies globally. Limited to 150 days — set to expire around July 24, 2026. Exceptions for USMCA goods, Section 232 products, and certain CAFTA-DR textiles.

Standard MFN duties

Regular Most Favored Nation tariff rates in the Harmonized Tariff Schedule. Vary by product, have been in effect for decades. Not changed by any recent action.

The net effect: The Treasury Secretary has stated that combining Section 122, 232, and 301 aims to produce "virtually unchanged tariff revenue in 2026." Lower costs overall are unlikely — but importers are legally owed back the IEEPA portion specifically.
Action plan

What to do this week

1

Pull your ACE entry data

Generate a report of all entries from February 2025 onward. Note liquidation status and dates. If you don't have ACE access, request it from your customs broker or file a FOIA with CBP.

2

Separate IEEPA from other tariffs

For each entry, identify HTS headings 9903.01.xx and calculate the IEEPA-specific portion versus S.232, S.301, and MFN duties. Our refund estimator can help with rough numbers.

3

File PSCs on unliquidated entries

Fastest refund path. Have your broker file Post-Summary Corrections through ACE/ABI immediately for all open entries.

4

File protests on liquidated entries

File CBP Form 19 protests before the 180-day deadline. Prioritize oldest entries first — their windows close soonest.

5

Enroll in ACH

All refunds are now electronic. Register at pay.gov if you haven't already.

6

Consider a CIT protective filing

For significant refund amounts (typically $50K+), consult a trade attorney about a protective action at the Court of International Trade. Filing fee is ~$400 and preserves claims if administrative channels fail.

7

Keep paying current tariffs

S.122, S.232, S.301, and MFN tariffs are still due. Failure to pay current duties will jeopardize your bond and import privileges.
